A well-organized category system makes it easier to understand where your money goes and set meaningful budgets.

## Start broad, refine later

Begin with 10-15 broad categories rather than trying to be overly specific:

- **Housing** — Rent/mortgage, property tax, insurance, maintenance
- **Transportation** — Gas, car payment, insurance, parking, public transit
- **Food** — Groceries, dining out, coffee shops
- **Utilities** — Electric, gas, water, internet, phone
- **Healthcare** — Insurance premiums, copays, prescriptions, dental
- **Entertainment** — Streaming, hobbies, events, games
- **Shopping** — Clothing, household items, electronics
- **Income** — Salary, freelance, interest, dividends

## Use subcategories

Categories support nesting. Create subcategories when a broad category needs more detail:

- Food
  - Groceries
  - Dining Out
  - Coffee & Snacks

Only add subcategories when you find yourself wanting to distinguish spending within a category. You can always add them later.

## Income vs. expense

Mark categories as **income** or **expense**. This separation powers accurate reports and analytics. Income categories typically include salary, freelance income, interest, and refunds.

## Tax-related categories

Flag categories as **tax-related** for end-of-year tax preparation. Common tax-relevant categories include charitable donations, business expenses, medical costs, and property taxes.

## Default payee categories

Assign a **default category** to frequently used payees. When you import or manually add a transaction with that payee, the category is pre-filled automatically. This saves time and improves consistency.

## Tips

- Avoid creating categories you rarely use — consolidate under broader ones
- Review uncategorized transactions monthly and create categories as patterns emerge
- Use the payee cleanup tools to merge duplicate payees and standardize names
